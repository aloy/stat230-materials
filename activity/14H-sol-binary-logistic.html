Binary Logistic Regression

Overview

In this activity you will practice how to interpret fitted logistic regression models using a sample from the Framingham Heart Study data set.

slides that the Framingham Heart Study is a long-term study of cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke, and other heart problems) that began in 1948. The study identified the idea that body measurements (blood pressure, cholesterol, etc) could predict cardiovascular disease. In this activity we will explore an expanded set of predictor variables to model whether a participant experienced coronary heart disease (CHD) in a 10-year window after their exam. The variables we will consider include:

  • age: age of participant (in years)
  • male: biological sex of participant (1 = male, 0 = female)
  • totChol: total cholesterol (mg/dL)
  • sysBP: systolic blood pressure (mm Hg)

A “simple” logistic regression model

Like in linear regression, we refer to a “simple” logistic regression model as one with a single predictor variable. Below is the output from fitting a simple logistic regression model with totChol as the predictor variable.

# A tibble: 2 × 5
  term        estimate std.error statistic  p.value
  <chr>          <dbl>     <dbl>     <dbl>    <dbl>
1 (Intercept) -2.89     0.230       -12.6  1.92e-36
2 totChol      0.00488  0.000926      5.27 1.38e- 7

Task 1. Interpret the coefficient for totChol in the context of this problem. In particular, explain what this coefficient tells us about the relationship between total cholesterol and the odds of experiencing coronary heart disease in a 10-year window.

TipSolution

The coefficient for totChol is approximately 0.00488. This means that for each additional 1 mg/dL increase in total cholesterol, the log odds of experiencing coronary heart disease in a 10-year window increases by about 0.00488, holding all other variables constant.

But… we don’t think on the log-odds scale, so we convert that to an odds ratio by exponentiating the coefficient: \(e^{0.00488.} \approx 1.005\).

A 1 mg/dL increase in total cholesterol is associated with an approximate 0.5% increase in the odds of experiencing coronary heart disease in a 10-year window.

Task 2. Using the model output above, calculate the predicted probability of experiencing coronary heart disease in a 10-year window for participants with the following total cholesterol levels:

  1. 240 mg/dL
  2. 250 mg/dL
  3. 210 mg/dL
  4. 220 md/dL
TipSolution

To calculate the predicted probability, we first need to calculate the log odds using the fitted logistic regression equation: \[\log \left( \dfrac{\widehat{\pi}}{1-\widehat{\pi}} \right) = -2.89 + 0.00488 \times \text{totChol}\] Then, we convert the log odds to a predicted probability using the formula: \[\widehat{\pi} = \dfrac{e^{\text{log odds}}}{1 + e^{\text{log odds}}}\]

  1. For totChol = 240 mg/dL:
  • Log odds: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 240 \approx -1.7188\)
  • Predicted probability: \(\dfrac{e^{-1.7188}}{1 + e^{-1.7188}} \approx 0.152\)
  1. For totChol = 250 mg/dL:
  • Log odds: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 250 \approx -1.67\)
  • Predicted probability: \(\dfrac{e^{-1.67}}{1 + e^{-1.67}} \approx 0.158\)
  1. For totChol = 210 mg/dL:
  • Log odds: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 210 \approx -1.8652\)
  • Predicted probability: \(\dfrac{e^{-1.8652}}{1 + e^{-1.8652}} \approx 0.134\)
  1. For totChol = 220 mg/dL:
  • Log odds: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 220 \approx -1.8164\)
  • Predicted probability: \(\dfrac{e^{-1.8164}}{1 + e^{-1.8164}} \approx 0.139\)

Task 3. Based on your calculations above, is the effect of a 10 mg/dL increase in total cholesterol on the predicted probability of experiencing coronary heart disease in a 10-year window constant?

TipSolution

No, the effect of a 10 mg/dL increase in total cholesterol on the predicted probability is not constant. From 240 to 250 mg/dL, the predicted probability increases from approximately 0.152 to 0.158, an increase of about 0.006. From 210 to 220 mg/dL, the predicted probability increases from approximately 0.134 to 0.139, an increase of about 0.005. The increase in predicted probability is slightly larger when moving from 240 to 250 mg/dL compared to moving from 210 to 220 mg/dL.

Task 4. Calculate the log odds ratio for an increase from 240 to 250 mg/dL in total cholesterol. Then, calculate the log odds ratio for an increase from 210 to 220 mg/dL in total cholesterol. Are these log odds ratios the same?

TipSolution

The log odds ratio for an increase from 240 to 250 mg/dL in total cholesterol is calculated as follows: - Log odds at 250 mg/dL: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 250 \approx -1.67\) - Log odds at 240 mg/dL: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 240 \approx -1.7188\) - Log odds ratio: \(-1.67 - (-1.7188) \approx 0.0488\)

The log odds ratio for an increase from 210 to 220 mg/dL in total cholesterol is calculated as follows: - Log odds at 220 mg/dL: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 220 \approx -1.8164\) - Log odds at 210 mg/dL: \(-2.89 + 0.00488 \times 210 \approx -1.8652\) - Log odds ratio: \(-1.8164 - (-1.8652) \approx 0.0488\)

Yes, these log odds ratios are the same, both approximately 0.0488. This indicates that the effect of a 10 mg/dL increase in total cholesterol on the log odds of experiencing coronary heart disease in a 10-year window is constant.

Hypothesis tests for individual regression coefficients

The regression coefficients from a logistic regression model are approximately normally distributed, so we can use the normal distribution to conduct hypothesis tests for individual regression coefficients. These tests are called Wald tests.

Task 11. Using the model output from mod4 above, let’s conduct a hypothesis test to determine whether the interaction between totChol and male is necessary.
